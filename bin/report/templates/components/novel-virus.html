Novel virus detection
  1. The evidence gathered under the Diamond method represents de novo assembled contigs that are at least 1000 bp, a query coverage > 50% and a percentage identity ranging between 50% and 90% to a viral sequence in the RVDB viral protein database.
  2. The evidence gathered under the Megablast method represents de novo assembled contigs that are at least 1000 bp, a percentage identity > 90% to a viral sequence in the NCBI core_nt database and have a 30X depth coverage of at least 70%.
  3. The evidence gathered under the geNomad method represents de novo assembled contigs that did not show significant similarity to any sequence in the NCBI core_nt database but have viral characteristics (based on geNomad and hmmscan). Candidate contigs were first classified using geNomad, a machine learning–based tool that evaluates gene content and sequence composition to predict viral sequences. Open reading frames (ORFs) encoding protein sequences of at least 100 amino acids were subsequently predicted using orfipy, and the translated protein sequences were screened against the curated Pfam-A profile Hidden Markov Model database using hmmscan. The table reports ORFs, the number of predicted ORFs with significant viral domain matches (defined as a full sequence score of at least 50); PFAM total, the total number of detected viral domains; Viral hallmark genes, the number of viral hallmark genes identified by geNomad , and RdRP, whether an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRP) domain was detected. Contigs are considered high confidence if they are at least 1000 bp in length, have a geNomad virus score (posterior probability of being viral) of at least 0.999 and present at least one of the following features: an RdRP domain, one or more viral PFAM domains or one or more viral hallmark genes.
  4. The evidence gathered under the Kraken and Kaiju methods represents unresolved taxonomic assignments at the family or order level, with at least 1000 reads support, providing further evidence supporting the presence of potentially novel or highly divergent viruses within the sample.
